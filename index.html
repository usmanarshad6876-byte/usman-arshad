A mixed-methods systematic review and meta-analysis examining whether culturally adapted parenting interventions improve outcomes for South Asian families — and critically, whether the depth of cultural adaptation matters. Searched CINAHL, MEDLINE, ScienceDirect, PsycINFO, PubMed, and Cochrane Library (up to August 2023). Evaluated 17 studies (15 quantitative, 2 qualitative; 10 in meta-analysis) across Pakistan, Bangladesh, India, and Norwegian South Asian diaspora. Used Bernal's Ecological Validity Model across 8 dimensions: language, persons, metaphors, content, concepts, goals, methods, and context.
Key Effect Sizes
SMD 0.51 — Parenting knowledge
SMD 0.36 — Parental involvement
SMD −0.77 — Parental depression (significant)
SMD 0.84–1.48 — Child cognitive development
SMD 0.79 — Child language development
SMD 0.54 — Child social development
Key finding on cultural adaptation: studies meeting 7 or more EVM adaptation domains showed stronger and more consistent effects, especially for child cognitive development. Deeper adaptation — local language, culturally familiar examples, traditional games and songs, pictorial materials for low-literacy families, home-based delivery, family involvement — outperforms surface-level translation. Qualitative synthesis found maternal confidence, empowerment, and trust improved when facilitators understood local family structures and norms.

Effectiveness of YCMAP (youth culturally adapted manual-assisted problem solving) in adolescents after self-harm in Pakistan: multicentre, randomised controlled trial
Co-author  ·  BMJ 2025  ·  DOI: 10.1136/bmj-2024-083272

A landmark two-arm multicentre RCT across Karachi, Hyderabad, Lahore, Multan, and Rawalpindi — the first large-scale trial of an adolescent self-harm intervention in Pakistan. Adolescents aged 12–18 with self-harm in the past three months were randomised to either YCMAP (up to 10 CBT-informed, culturally adapted sessions delivered by trained psychology graduates, available in English and Urdu) plus enhanced usual care, or enhanced usual care alone. Primary result: 3 repeat self-harm events in the YCMAP group vs 15 in controls at 12 months (OR 0.20). YCMAP also produced significant early improvements in distress, hopelessness, and suicidal ideation at 3 months. Strikingly, 88% of recruited adolescents reported self-harm with intent to die, and 79% had not told anyone — illustrating the hidden scale of adolescent suicide risk in Pakistan. Culturally critical features included optional family sessions, Urdu-language materials, and sensitivity to stigma, religious context, and family dynamics. Intervention is scalable through non-specialist therapists under supervision — directly relevant for workforce-limited LMIC health systems.
Cities: Karachi · Hyderabad · Lahore · Multan · Rawalpindi  |  Languages: English & Urdu  |  Follow-up: 12 months

A systematic review and meta-analysis of neuroimaging studies examining synaptic density in individuals with psychotic spectrum disorders
Co-author (3rd) — PILL & University of Manchester  ·  PROSPERO: CRD42022359018
The first systematic review and meta-analysis of in vivo neuroimaging studies specifically examining synaptic density across the psychosis spectrum. Searched MEDLINE, Embase, PsycINFO, Web of Science and Cochrane Library from inception to December 2023. Of 3,199 studies identified, 5 met inclusion criteria (n=128 adults; 61 psychotic disorder, 67 healthy controls). Meta-analysis found significantly reduced synaptic density — measured via PET imaging of SV2A glycoprotein — across multiple brain regions including occipital lobe, temporal lobe, parietal lobe, anterior cingulate cortex, frontal cortex, amygdala, thalamus, and hippocampus. Multilingual Scholarship & Cultural Adaptation

Where language, culture, and
evidence meet.

Why This Work Is Necessary

Pakistan has over 220 million people and fewer than 400 psychiatrists. An estimated 14% of the population — roughly 30 million people — live with a mental health condition. The vast majority receive no care at all. This is the treatment gap.

But the treatment gap is not just a numbers problem. It is also a knowledge problem, a language problem, and a cultural problem. Most of the world's mental health evidence is generated in high-income, Western, English-speaking contexts — and then exported to settings where it was never tested, in languages communities do not speak, through frameworks that do not reflect local understandings of distress, family, faith, or healing.

An intervention developed in Manchester or New York and delivered unchanged in Lahore or Quetta is not evidence-based practice. It is assumption. Closing the treatment gap requires more than scale — it requires evidence that actually fits the people it is meant to help.

Pakistan is not linguistically uniform. Punjabi is spoken by around 44% of the population, Pashto by 15%, Sindhi by 14%, Balochi by 4% — yet health information is routinely produced only in Urdu or English, the native languages of a small minority. Communities in Balochistan, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, interior Sindh, and rural Punjab are systematically excluded from mental health knowledge — not because the knowledge does not exist, but because no one has put it in a form they can access.

Cultural adaptation is not a softening of rigour — it is a prerequisite for it. Concepts like 'self', 'depression', 'trauma', and 'recovery' carry different meanings across Pakistani linguistic and religious communities. Interventions that ignore this do not just underperform — they can actively cause harm by pathologising normal cultural experience or dismissing protective cultural frameworks.

This is the problem Usman's work addresses. Not as a peripheral interest, but as a thread running through his thesis, his systematic reviews, his policy contributions, and the newsletter he built — an institution-level commitment to the principle that evidence must be culturally grounded and linguistically accessible, or it will never reach the people who need it most.

Newsletters & Knowledge Translation

Founded & led for over a decade.

Usman established the PILL Newsletter from the ground up and has led it for over ten years — building it into Pakistan's most comprehensive multilingual mental health newsletter series. What began as an institutional communication initiative became a nationally distributed knowledge translation platform, translating complex research into accessible public health information across all four provinces in 7 languages, reaching communities from Balochistan to Khyber Pakhtunkhwa.
